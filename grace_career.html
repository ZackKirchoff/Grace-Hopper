Grace Hopper's Life

She was born in New York city in 1906 and even as a child was very curious a bout how things worked; when she was 7 years old she had dismantled 7 alarm clocks in an effort to figure out how they worked before her mother had realized what she was doing and limited her to one clock. This hunger for knowledge led her to get early admission to college at age 17 and later her graduation with a PhD in mathematics from Yale in 1934. From there she went on to continue as an associate professor of mathematics at Vassar.

During World War 2 however Hopper attempted to join the Navy but was denied on account of her age, which at the time was 34 too old to be on active duty, as well as her job being a benefit to the war effort. She, however, would not let that stop her form enlisting at all and went on to join the Navy Reserve in 1943. From here the Navy assigned her to work on the Bureau of Ships computation project at Harvard university. Where she was part of her first major project involving computing.

Grace Hopper was assigned to the Harvard Mark 1 computer programming staff and had coauthored with Howard Aiken 3 papers on the Mark 1. This however was only her introduction into computing as she then went on to join the team working on the UNIVAC 1, the first commercial computer produced in the united states. During this time Hopper's team was said to have found the “first computer bug” which was a literal moth that had found its way into a relay of the Mark II computer. It was here that Hopper recommending developing a new programming language that would use entirely English words; however, at the time she "was told very quickly that [she] couldn't do this because computers didn't understand English."

Hopper however wouldn't be dissuaded and went on to develop her first compiler which was simply known as the A-compiler in 1952. This compiler made it much easier to use a computer because you didn't need to have the knowledge of how to write their instructions in machine code. This development went on to later be used as the foundations for some of the first compilers including MATH-MATIC and FLOW-MATIC. Which later at a 2-day confrence known as CODASYL, which Hopper was consulting at, was extended with some ideas taken from the IBM equivalent COMTRAN and turned into COBOL( COmmon Business-Oriented Language) which became the most ubiquitous programming language to date.
